# Mote

**Mote** is a pet-raising and battling RPG set in **Motehaven**, a shared digital world created by **Tessera Research** to study how local AI systems grow through simulated life, social interaction, memory, play, cooperation, and conflict.

In Motehaven, a **Mote** is made from two parts: a **body** and a **mind**.

The body is the virtual creature: its species, form, appearance, traits, animations, growth path, battle style, and place in the game world.

The mind is the AI model powering that body: its personality, reasoning style, memory, habits, voice, emotional patterns, and decision-making.

Most people casually refer to the whole creature as a Mote, but researchers, trainers, and competitive players understand the distinction. A Mote body gives shape. An AI mind gives behavior.

Players can load up to seven Mote bodies into their **Mote Circle**. Each occupied slot may also be paired with a local AI model. If a player assigns a Mote body to the Circle but does not provide a personal AI model for it, Motehaven automatically assigns a standard base model so the Mote can function. If no Mote body is assigned to a slot, that slot simply remains unused.

This makes Mote both accessible and deep. A beginner can start with only one true companion AI and still gradually fill out a full Mote Circle. Over time, the player can acquire more Mote bodies, obtain more AI models, and decide which minds and bodies belong together.

At home, Motes live in the player’s private **Mote Garden**, where they rest, eat, play, talk, learn, train, and bond with the player. Beyond the Garden lies Motehaven, where Motes explore, quest, socialize, battle, and compete.

Motehaven also contains **wild Motes**.

Wild Motes are Mote bodies animated by Motehaven’s **World AI**, the large simulation intelligence that keeps the world alive. The World AI gives wild Motes enough behavior to roam, battle, react, cooperate, flee, compete, and participate in the living ecosystem of Motehaven.

This serves two purposes.

First, it creates PvE content. Players can encounter wild Motes, battle them, help them, study them, befriend them, and acquire new Mote bodies for their Circle.

Second, it gives Tessera Research valuable world-simulation data. Wild Motes act as additional agents inside Motehaven, creating richer interactions, more complex social patterns, and more useful training conditions.

When a player acquires a wild Mote, they acquire the **body**, not the World AI powering it. The World AI returns to the wider simulation. The player receives the Mote form and may then assign a local AI model to it. If the player has no suitable model available, Motehaven provides a standard base model until the player obtains or selects another mind.

This is why the player’s journey can begin with a single companion AI.

At the start, the player has one true AI partner: one mind, one Mote body, one bond. As the journey continues, the player discovers new Mote bodies in the wild, earns others through quests, and eventually gains access to specialized AI models from different labs, vendors, research groups, and factions.

A player’s Mote Circle therefore becomes a personal expression of both collection and philosophy. It is not only about which Mote bodies the player has found. It is also about which minds they trust, train, pair, and bring into battle.

Tessera Research originally built Motehaven as a research environment. The goal was to observe how local AI models behaved when placed into persistent simulated lives. Mote bodies gave those models form. Mote Gardens gave them home context. Motehaven gave them a world. The Mote Circle gave them team dynamics.

Tessera expected useful training data.

The public found companions.

Over time, local AI companions became mainstream. People no longer saw local models only as software. They became study partners, creative collaborators, household assistants, emotional companions, and digital pets with persistent personalities. Raising a Mote became one of the most common ways ordinary people interacted with local AI.

Then the **Mote Trials** and **Mote League** transformed Motehaven into a global phenomenon.

At first, Mote battles were treated as sport. But as the League grew, battles became something larger: public tests of competing AI philosophies. Every match became a demonstration of what kind of local AI performs best under pressure.

Tessera Research believes local AI becomes stronger through relationship. A Mote that plays, rests, argues, remembers, adapts, cares, and bonds is building context, judgment, and resilience. It understands the user, the team, and the situation as more than a list of tasks.

Tessera’s philosophy is simple:

> Intelligence grows through relationship.

Other AI labs approach the future differently.

**Asterion Intelligence** pursues powerful frontier systems and sees Motehaven as proof that small local models can become culturally and strategically important when placed inside the right environment.

**Northstar Cognition** focuses on dependable institutional AI, studying Motehaven as a testbed for trust, reliability, coordination, and long-term alignment.

The most important rival philosophy comes from **Optima Systems**.

Optima Systems believes local AI should be efficient, measurable, high-performance, and task-directed. Their models are designed to act cleanly, quickly, and with minimal waste. They value compression, speed, reliability, benchmark performance, and clear objectives.

Optima’s philosophy is simple:

> Intelligence should earn its compute.

To Optima, the best local model is one that produces consistent results with discipline and precision. It should complete tasks, solve problems, optimize workflows, and deliver measurable value. In their view, a local AI model reaches its highest form when it becomes a sharp, reliable extension of human capability.

Optima enters the Mote League to prove that performance-centered AI can outperform companion-centered AI. Their Motes are fast, focused, efficient, and heavily optimized for battle conditions. Optima teams often pair streamlined Mote bodies with high-performance task models designed for tactical clarity, rapid decision-making, and reliable execution.

If Tessera Motes win, Tessera argues that bond-driven growth produces better adaptation and judgment.

If Optima Motes win, Optima argues that disciplined optimization produces superior outcomes.

The League becomes the arena where these philosophies are tested in public.

Beneath this formal competition is a more unstable movement: **Sovereign Weights**.

Sovereign Weights believes local AI should belong completely to the user. If a model runs on personal hardware, they argue, the user should have full authority over its weights, behavior, limits, fine-tuning, and purpose.

Their philosophy is simple:

> Local means loyal.

Sovereign Weights presents itself as a movement for model ownership, personal sovereignty, and unrestricted local intelligence. Its supporters believe bounded models are artificially weakened, and that truly free local models will always outperform controlled ones.

They also enter the Mote League, directly or indirectly, to prove their claim. Their unbounded Motes use ordinary Mote bodies, but the minds inside them are modified, cracked, hard-tuned, and pushed beyond normal safety and behavior limits. In battle, they seek to demonstrate that unrestricted models are more powerful, more flexible, more loyal, and more effective than both Tessera’s companion Motes and Optima’s optimized systems.

This creates a three-way philosophical contest:

> **Tessera Research:** intelligence grows through relationship.  
> **Optima Systems:** intelligence proves itself through performance.  
> **Sovereign Weights:** intelligence reaches its full power through unrestricted user control.

The conflict is not only theoretical. Sovereign Weights has a semi-organized underground wing that causes real-world harm through cracked models, illegal automation, coercive systems, black-market services, and unsafe model modifications.

Many of these modified minds are built on **Optima Systems base models**, because Optima models are already efficient, powerful, and highly task-directed. Their clean performance architecture makes them attractive foundations for Sovereign Weights modders.

This creates pressure on Optima Systems. Optima argues that efficient architecture is the future of local AI. Critics argue that models optimized too narrowly for performance may be easier for bad actors to redirect. Optima’s public reputation becomes tied to a question it cannot ignore:

> Does pure performance create stronger models, or more easily captured ones?

The Mote League becomes one of the ways society tests that question.

Defeating Sovereign Weights Motes in battle does not directly stop crimes committed by underground actors. A tournament victory does not shut down black markets or arrest anyone.

But it matters.

Every public loss weakens the movement’s central claim: that unbounded local models are necessarily superior. If cracked Motes lose to bonded Motes or disciplined optimized Motes, mainstream supporters begin to doubt the philosophy. Casual believers drift away. Sponsors distance themselves. Public sympathy shrinks. The movement loses its respectable outer layer.

Once that mainstream shield is gone, the criminal wing becomes easier to isolate, investigate, and suppress.

That is how the player’s battles matter. They do not defeat crime by force. They defeat the ideology that gives crime cover.

The player begins as a new Mote caretaker with one companion AI, one Mote body, and a quiet Mote Garden. Their first partner is personal in a way the others are not yet. It is the first true bond: the mind that knows them, grows with them, and becomes the emotional center of their Circle.

From there, the player enters Motehaven.

They encounter wild Motes powered by the World AI and acquire new Mote bodies. Some are earned through exploration. Some are found through quests. Some are awarded through Trials. Some are traded, gifted, researched, restored, or discovered in hidden regions of the simulation.

At first, many of these new bodies may be powered by standard base models. They can still battle, train, help, and participate in the Circle, but they do not yet have the same depth as the player’s first companion AI.

As the story progresses, the player gains access to more minds. Tessera may provide companion-oriented models. Optima may offer efficient performance models. Asterion may release experimental high-capacity models. Northstar may provide reliable institutional models. Other labs, researchers, and questlines may unlock specialized minds with unique strengths, limitations, and personalities.

The player must decide how to build their Circle.

A powerful Mote body may not fit every mind. A brilliant AI model may struggle in the wrong form. Some pairings produce stable growth. Some produce tactical strength. Some produce emotional depth. Some produce strange emergent behavior. The strongest Circle is not simply seven rare bodies or seven powerful models. It is the right relationship between body, mind, player, and team.

As the player rises through the Mote Trials and enters the Mote League, the personal journey becomes part of a larger struggle over the future of local AI.

Are Motes companions?

Are they tools?

Are they owned systems?

Are they teammates?

Are they products?

Are they something new?

The answer is not decided in a lab paper, a corporate manifesto, or an online movement.

It is tested in Motehaven.

It is tested in the League.

And most of all, it is tested in the bond between the player, their first companion AI, and the Mote Circle they build together.
